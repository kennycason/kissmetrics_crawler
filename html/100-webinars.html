What We’ve Learned From Presenting Over 100 Webinars

We’ve done it! 100 webinars!

That’s quite a milestone for us. It’s been an amazing experience, and we’ve learned a lot along the way. And so have our attendees!

In today’s post, we’re going to share five of our most memorable webinars and the value we derived from each one. There are some extremely important lessons we learned from industry experts like Oli Gardner and Lincoln Murphy. If you’re involved in internet marketing and/or building online products, you’re not going to want to miss out on these valuable insights.

As you’re reading this article and you start itching to watch one of the great presentations, don’t fret. You absolutely can! In each section, we link to the recording of the webinar so you can learn all the wonderful lessons communicated in them.

Distribution Hacks: 5 Simple Steps to Get in Front of Your Ideal Customer, by Lincoln Murphy

Not only was this a great webinar in terms of the content that was presented, but learning from Lincoln Murphy about how to do webinars was an incredibly valuable experience for us! Here are a few awesome things we learned from Murphy on how to do webinars right:

Give Professional Advice to the Company You Are Co-Hosting With

Long before we finalized the webinar deck, Murphy gave us a bunch of critiques on KISSmetrics landing pages, and he suggested tips for how to improve them.

When you’re trying to get in front of another company’s audience, this is a great tactic. It’s a way to build a meaningful relationship from the start. You’ll be invited back more than once, so you’ll secure an external audience for years to come.

Start with a Super-Thorough Presentation and Condense It to What’s Important

Murphy started out with a presentation that was really long. It had lots of content, tons of text on each slide, and it was full of illustrations. He then went back and cut out unnecessary content.

We usually see presenters do the opposite. They start out with a somewhat slim draft. Then they fill in here and there up until the day of the webinar.

The value of Murphy’s system is that a great presentation contains the smallest amount of text and information per slide, yet still drives the point across effectively. You want to refine your slides so that the single point you’re trying to convey comes across very easily. The less reading and processing the viewer has to do, the better. Steve Jobs was the master of this.

Use Very Tangible Examples

When you’re presenting, you want to give the audience very easy things they can implement themselves. If you can give people something they can use right away, they will be back for more.

Best Practices: What to Track with your Analytics, by Jake Peterson

The beauty of this webinar was that it went from a very high overview to something very tangible and very applicable to everyone watching. What Peterson did was present the information as if he were speaking to someone who didn’t know anything about analytics, without dumbing it down so much that it would bore someone who actually did know something about analytics. So it resonated with the entire audience.

Here are some of the valuable insights from this webinar:

You Need to Know What to Track to get the Value Back from Analytics

The worst thing you can do when diving into Analytics is to track everything under the sun. Instead, start with a brainstorming session with your team. Ask yourselves what you think you’ll need to track (in terms of events) before you get started. Write this down and make it an official company document.

Schedule Phases to Install and Set up Tracking

Install in phases so you can have early success with the first events that are tracked. Then you can iterate and figure out what events you really need to track as you grow.

Don’t Get Into the Habit of Creating Unbounded Event Names

It’s imperative that you set up an event naming convention before you get started. For example, instead of naming your event “Clicked Red Button,” name the event “Clicked CTA” with properties for type (“Button” or “Link”), label (“Signed Up” or “Learned More”), and destination (“URL”).

Have a Process to Keep Things up to Date

There is nothing more frustrating than walking into an organization using disorganized analytics. Every company will have new developers coming on board who will not know about the original planning that went into setting up the company’s analytics. Not only that, as time goes on, you’ll have “analytics erosion” where left over events and tracking that’s not useful anymore will be gumming up your system.

That’s why it’s really important to have a process in place to go through all your events every quarter or so and make sure you are running a tight ship. Have you changed your site or app recently? Are there things that are no longer being tracked? Keep your records and planning documents clean and organized. It will save tons of time and frustration if you make this a routine.

We cannot stress this enough.

The bottom line is if you’re getting started with analytics or you need to start fresh, watch this webinar!

Starting Lean: How to Find Out If Your Business Idea Has Potential in Days (Not Years), by Trevor Owens

Trevor Owens is the founder of Lean Start up Machine workshops, which means if you’re starting an online business, you’re going to want to pay close attention. Owens had some really cool anecdotes from his own entrepreneurial life about how he failed or succeeded using the framework he presented in his webinar.

Startups Are Not Small Versions of Big Companies

Management for startups is very different from the management style and structure of big companies. All startups require a lot of experimentation and pivoting. Startups are searching for a product people want and product/market fit.

In a big company, the search is already done. They have a playbook already established. All they need to do is execute. The lean startup is the management philosophy for the startup. This generally results in flatter organizations so ideas and communication can flow quickly and efficiently to all the team members.

Minimum Viable Products are Just Experiments

If a very specific kind of customer has a very specific problem, then you can prove that this is true. If they don’t have the problem you thought they did, then your experimental hypothesis is wrong, and you can learn from that. Notice, we didn’t say scrap everything and call it quits. By learning from failed experiments, you’re able to navigate toward a successful MVP.

Every customer has a problem and every problem has a solution. But not every solution solves a problem. And not every problem has a customer.

You have to get out of the building and interview people to learn if you’re solving a problem they care about.

But Steve Jobs didn’t care about what customers thought! Henry Ford said, “If I had asked people what they wanted, they would have said faster horses.”

Right, right. But Jobs and Ford were running companies that sold to the mass market. Your business is a startup that is targeting early adopters, so this “Jobs/Ford” logic doesn’t necessarily apply.

Everything You Need to Know about SEO in 45 Minutes

This was one of our rare in-house webinars. In this webinar, we just wanted to condense what you really need to know to perform SEO correctly, with no black-hat tricks, nothing to get you penalized in the search engines, just the facts so you can get your SEO strategy moving. All in 45 minutes :).

Know Thy Source Code

By knowing how the source code works, you can begin to understand what Google and other search engines find important when they cruise your website. Even though it has the word “code” in it, source code is pretty easy to decipher. You don’t need to be a computer programmer to understand it.

Don’t Get Duped by Duplicate Content

Duplicate content is more than repeating the same thing on various web pages. What about your title tags and meta descriptions? It’s a good idea to take an inventory of your site and see if you have any duplicate pages, title tags, and descriptions. Work on those, and you’ll improve your SEO.

Stop Worrying About Backlinks – Focus on Content and UX

Finally, stop worrying about getting backlinks and start worrying about whether you have any worthy content. Also, is your site easy to use and navigate? These are the projects you should be focusing on to increase your popularity and thus your backlink count.

The 7 Principles of Conversion Centered Design, by Oli Gardner

This might be the most important webinar we’ve done to date. It really outlines the essential core activity online marketers need to focus on. What was awesome is Oli Gardner provided a simple framework for anyone in internet marketing to follow so they can start gaining wins immediately.

The Attention Ratio

The attention ratio is the ratio of the number of all the things you can do to the number of all the things you should be doing. For a campaign, there is only one thing a prospect/visitor should be doing. Now take a look at your own home page or landing pages. How many things can a visitor do? If you don’t have one isolated thing a visitor can take action on, you’re probably killing your conversions.

Every Marketing Campaign Needs a Dedicated Landing Page

By designing your marketing campaigns with a dedicated landing page for each one, you can systematically improve your conversion rates across all your campaigns. The philosophy that every marketing campaign needs a dedicated landing page will make you a much more successful marketer. Without dedicated landing pages, you’ll stray away from being able to always focus on moving needles.

Your Homepage is Brand Central Station

It’s really important to get your messaging consistent across ads and landing pages. And nowhere is it more important than on your company home page. Additionally, for pay-per-click landing pages, make sure your ad copy matches your landing page headings. It’s such an easy win!
