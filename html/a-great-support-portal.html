The Must-Have Elements of a Great Support Portal

Most businesses work hard to ensure their products look great on their website, but very few expend the same effort to set up a comprehensive support portal. This is mostly because businesses are more focused on acquiring customers than retaining them.

However, it’s not just marketing content that generates revenue. Great product documentation can go a long way toward keeping your customers and improving your chances of getting return business. It can also make a huge difference for your support team that is dealing with issues and trying to make your customers happy every day.

That said, building a support portal is easier said than done. There are many things you can do to make sure your users don’t feel stranded, so we decided to put together a list to help you stay on track.

Tell Customers the Best Way to Reach You

Customers have different ways of getting in touch with you. Some prefer email, some prefer live-chat, some prefer to tweet, and some… Well, some just want their problems to be solved without saying a word.

So, take a leaf out of Harry’s book: make your contact number and email easy to find by putting it right on top of your help center. This way, your users know every channel they can use to get in touch with you, and they can pick the one that suits them best.

State Clearly When Support Is Available

Imagine this: your customers are in a total state of panic. Your service has been down for the last five minutes, and they’re in complete hysteria mode trying to reach you. But, your support team in California has called it a day early because it’s a holiday. The next shift hasn’t taken over yet, so your customers are going to have wait for a response. Every minute you’re not responding, a customer is ranting about you on Twitter, or worse, switching to your competitor.

By being transparent about exactly when your support team is available, you can set expectations correctly and make sure your users don’t needlessly keep checking back. To avoid unwanted frustration, make sure you have pre-defined SLAs, and take business hours and holidays into account properly. Take a cue from MailChimp that usually lets users know with a banner when support is limited. You’ll find that it’s okay if you are up-front with your customers and tell them your team is going to be on vacation. Don’t worry, they won’t hate you for it.

Provide a Search Box in Your Support Portal

If you’re on a mission to alienate all your customers and lose all your fans, the easiest (and fastest) way to do so is to make articles in your support portal difficult to find. A search feature goes a long way toward helping customers quickly discover instructions and workarounds that might be otherwise buried in your knowledge base.

Not to mention, search options can help surface articles that might take hours to find, reducing anxiety when customers are already neck deep fighting problems and issues.

Also, there is the added boon of insight for you. Through your customers’ search terms, you’ll be able to determine the kinds of things they search for, what troubles them the most, and the different terms they use for your features. With that kind of data, you can circle back and improve the usability of your product.

Get Feedback on Your Help Content

It has happened to every one of us. After months of effort, we build a comprehensive, all-knowing knowledge base that has an answer to every question a customer could possibly have. We expect to bask in the glory of reduced ticket volume, happier agents, and even happier customers. But, the phones still ring, the customers still ping, and the emails still come in.

Our customers don’t love our cheat sheets as much as we do. They might find the instructions too vague or too detailed. (No one appreciates instructions like “Click the big rectangle that has Submit written on it.”) They might think our screenshots don’t explain the solution clearly.

There is no way to find out about these things if you don’t ask your customers if they find your articles useful.

The best way to do this is to add a feedback button to each of your articles. Based on the feedback, you can go back to the drawing board once a month and revamp your guides if a lot of customers report they don’t understand them.

Optimize Your Support Portal for Mobile Devices

By not optimizing your portal for mobile devices, you are at risk of alienating a sizeable target audience. This is 2015, when everyone is used to thumb-typing their way around the Internet and getting things done with their phone. When people are looking for better ways to take selfies, zoomed out desktop-size icons just aren’t worth their time. You cannot expect people to take the effort and navigate around, let alone be happy with your content.

If your support portal isn’t responsive for mobile yet, stop reading this article and go talk to your web developer right now. Something as simple as making your site mobile-friendly will mean all the difference to your customers.

Set Up a Status Page for When Things Go Wrong

You can have some of the best engineers in the world on your staff, but that won’t make you exempt from Murphy’s Law. What can go wrong will go wrong, and the first place your customers will go to find a solution is your support portal.

So, save your customers (and yourself) a world of trouble. Set up a status page that your customers can check to find out if something’s up with your service. Not only will you be saving yourself an avalanche of queries, but you’ll also earn brownie points for being transparent, even on your tough days.

Google does this very well. Businesses that use their suite internally can go to the App Status Dashboard to find out if any of their services are down and how severe the outage is. Product by product, day by day, it doesn’t get more reassuring than this.

Display The Local Time in Your HQ

This is kind of similar to point one, but is easier. Instead of having to manually adjust times when support is available and choosing to show it, you can choose to display the local time in your headquarters right on your support page. This works great if you have a single office or if you haven’t spread out your support team remotely (to deal with issues being reported by customers across different timezones).

Wistia does this really well by displaying the time in their headquarters in big, bold fonts right above the fold on their support page. They even go all the way to show off their support queue so users can have some clarity on how busy their team is.

Conclusion

All of this might seem kind of obvious to you; but when you’re setting up a support portal, it’s ridiculously easy to get lost by looking at the big picture. That can cause you to miss the small details. So, make sure you bookmark this checklist for the next time you decide to give your support portal a makeover. You’ll be surprised at the changes a ripple can make.
